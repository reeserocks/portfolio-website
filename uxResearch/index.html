User Experience Research

Project Introduction

In this research endeavor, I worked alongside a group of five other researchers over the course of a semester to investigate the effectiveness of digital media environment designs, with a focus on Wayfair and other online furniture retailers. We did competitive research, ran usability tests on the Wayfair site, conducted in-depth interviews about online furniture shopping in general, then worked to develop personas and other tools based on our findings.

01 Heuristic Analysis

After taking a good look at the Wayfair site, my team & I determined three user goals that would guide our analysis. Clear user goals helped us evaluate the site from the eyes of consumers, not designers.

From there, we each individually analyzed the site using resources such as the Forrester scorecard, and I learned what usability heuristics were and how to apply them. We then synthesized our analyses as a group, determining that the most critical issues could be found from heuristics with the lowest scores.

Finding: Wayfair suffers from information overload— it violates Psychological Usability Heuristics 1.2 "Progressive Disclosure" & 6.1 "Users' attention is grabbed and held in the right moments," revealing too much information to the user at once in an overwhelming manner, such as a long list of categories to sort through.

02 Competitive UX Research

Gathering Data

As a group, we brainstormed five dimensions (in addition to our three user goals) to evaluate six online retailer competitors against. We compiled our findings into a shared Google Sheet, noting what makes the competitors stand out against Wayfair.

Analyzing Data

Individually, I then wrote a 2-page paper about the strengths and weaknesses of competitor sites, thinking about how Wayfair can stand out against these brands. Through this analysis, I learned more about market trends and business strategy.

Wayfair has an opportunity to stand out from the competition due to its depth of inventory in the specific category of furniture. This concentration paves the way for features like Wayfair's specific filtering system, letting users sort by highly specific dimensions, materials, room types, style, and more. However, this strength is also a weakness. The number of product options can feel overwhelming, especially when listed so closely side-by-side like they are.

03 Usability Testing

Process

Now that we had a good idea of what we were looking to research about Wayfair, we proceeded into user interviews. We decided that our selection criteria would not be very strict, as most modern people have partaken in online shopping. All of us chose people we knew well, such as roommates or friends, so that the conversational dialogue we were aiming for with the testing script would be more easily achievable. Our young ages also meant that our interviewees had never used the Wayfair site before, granting us richer and useful first impressions of the site.

In class, we spent a lot of time practicing the "think-aloud" protocol, which reminds interviewees to explain their thoughts and the actions they are taking. I learned that this would be the most difficult part to upkeep during user interviews, as it is very common for interviewees to forget to think out loud.

I also learned how to ask better questions— for example, staying away from leading questions and letting the user fill in the latter half of my half-formed question themself.

Results

With six people on our team, we ended up with six videos ranging from 20 minutes to 1 hour long. In order to glean quantitative data from the interviews, we created a tally spreadsheet, where we defined success criteria to help us breakdown where the users passed without problems, took wrong turns, and/or failed a scenario.

The most interesting data emerged from the "Design Tools" scenario, where users were meant to find tools to give them inspiration and guide them through the design process. We thought they would run to the AI tool Wayfair has, but only one user managed to find the tool, even when multiple users were looking for it. In a digital landscape dominated by AI, we thought Wayfair should do a better job of advocating for its emerging tech tools.

To wrap up our takeaways, we presented our findings, backed by real evidence from user interviews, like we were speaking to the Wayfair company.

Example Finding

Users have trouble finding design tools on Wayfair because no clear path is given. All users struggled in some way to find a design tool, and all users took different paths to finding one of the tools available to help inspire their design. Evidence: 3 users completely failed to complete their task and find inspiration, and 2 users required prompting in order to aid them in completing the task.

04 In-Depth Interviews

With the in-depth interviews, we moved away from researching Wayfair specifically and shifted into the greater e-commerce space. We were pickier in selecting interviewees this time, as we wanted people who had actually bought furniture online before, so our age demographic tilted older. Three research questions defined our inquiries, asking what users find important while online shopping, what online tools they use, and what issues they encounter.

Moderating this kind of interview was much like the usability test, but I needed to be much more careful in how I asked questions, as I didn't have any sort of actionable tasks to fall back on— just the interviewee's life experiences. We wanted to ask questions that would illicit emotional, raw responses, so my team made sure to write down questions in our loose script that would prompt the interviewee to tell a real, multifaceted story.

During the online interview, I used the AI-powered extension Tactiq to transcribe the meeting, then double-checked it with the footage I had screen recorded. The next step was to code the interview, which was the most confusing part of this research project for me, as quantitative data is easier for my INTJ brain to comprehend. Practicing coding an unrelated interview as prep work luckily helped me overcome this struggle.

From what we learned in the IDI and by making all the artifacts shown below, we presented our findings and redesign recommendations to the class as if we were speaking to the Wayfair company.

Affinity Diagramming

Affinity diagramming with sticky notes felt different than typing the codes digitally, since it was easier to visually see categories and move notes around when categories were too broad. As a team, we determined the most important emerging themes to be:

  • Mixing Online & Offline Shopping;
  • Importance of Reviews & Well-Designed Filtering Tools;
  • Expectation–Experience Mismatch.

These themes touched on critical quotes from our interviews and encompassed the categories that had the most codes associated with them. After we were done synthesizing themes, our professor shared an AI summary of our combined interviews, which helped us define the titles of our themes, but did not really help us discover groundbreaking new information.

Persona Development

We started with three user personas, each one derived from a different type of user shown in our IDI research. Then, we narrowed it down from there and ended up with Nick Hammond, a recent grad with the goal to decorate his bare apartment.

We went with Nick over our other option of Joanna, a family-oriented rich homeowner, because we felt his limited budget and undetermined personal style matched Wayfair's big deals and assortment of design tools to help users define their aesthetic.

After designing Nick's persona on Canva, we shared it with another team in the class for critique. With their feedback in mind, we scrapped a useless "decision-making" section and wrote more about his personal life outside of shopping on Wayfair.

Empathy Mapping

Like the persona, our empathy map was created from a blend of each of our individual takes on Nick's words, thoughts, actions, and feelings. We combined notes that were similar and got rid of notes that didn't feel like they truly aligned with Nick's goals.

Distinguishing "thinks" from "says" was a fun challenge, as it is true that people do not often say exactly what they are thinking, so Nick's thoughts are more vulnerable.

Some of his "does" notes are taken from actions we saw during our usability tests, or modified from conversations we had during in-depth interviews.

Above all else, we wanted Nick to truly reflect all the data we had captured and not "data" we imagined.

Journey Mapping

Unlike other artifacts we created from the in-depth interviews, the journey map did not have as much prep work behind it, emerging from what we learned from readings instead of a combination of individual work.

When creating the journey map, we considered the most emotional and relevant parts of Nick's persona. We thought including his mother in the discovery phase to be realistic and relatable to similar users. Filters/reviews were another very crucial aspect we wanted to touch on. From there, we included other minor issues we encountered, such as an interrupted checkout flow and very few in-person store return options.

Finding: Mixing Online & Offline Shopping— Our interviewees tend to prefer in-person shopping for larger or more expensive pieces and online shopping for the convenience. Many shoppers mixed these experiences, utilizing online shopping for more varied furniture options and for more time to decide on an item after leaving the store.

Redesign Recommendation: Allow users to create curated lists of furniture online from in-store locations near them, so that we can help users prepare the items they want to look at when they enter the store.

05 Card Sort, Tree Tests, and First-Click Testing

Moving away from Wayfair now and looking at methodologies used by the field of UX Research as a whole, I participated in three open card sorts.

As an introduction to the topic, one of these card sorts was an online unmoderated card sort. I learned about the challenges of categorizing information without knowing the labels, but how, as a researcher, you learn a lot more about how your users think that way. In my research on the topic of information hierarchies, I also read about tree tests and first-click testing.

The other two card sorts were moderated, where for one session I was the moderator, then transitioned into a participant. As the moderator, I set out to observe areas of difference among the participant group, identify content that didn't make sense, and think about how different types of participants view content. From their proposed card sort, my team created a web navigation scheme.

Content Quality Assessment

Another UX methodology we utilized was evaluating a company's content strategy approach. To practice, my team & I looked at Student Life pages of prestigious universities, identifying the tone of the page and if that matched to what their branding should be. I learned how much page content influences tone, not just design choices, but even the fact of if people in a picture are smiling or not.

Circling back on Wayfair research, I audited Wayfair's homepage content using Content Science Review's Content Quality Checklist. I found that all of Wayfair's content is there, but the site gets lost in the voice & style category. For example, vocabulary like "staycation" and "vibes" don't make much sense to use when trying to appeal to homeowners, a demographic that is largely not Gen-Z.

Design Persona

Like the persona before it, this design persona was built off of each team member creating their own unique design persona. Some went more in the direction of a friendly neighbor, while others went in a more AI-focused direction, but we decided on an HGTV-inspired stylish and inspirational design persona, since Wayfair fits these personality traits the most.

I learned a lot about how even the smallest copy, such as error messages, can be a great place to explore tone and emphasize branding.
